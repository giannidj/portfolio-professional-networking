Tech & Meet – Cyber Threat Intelligence

On October 7th, I attended a free Tech & Meet evening session focused on Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI), presented by Niels Desloover and Sandro Manzo from the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium (CCB).

The event was aimed at introducing participants to the concept of threat intelligence and showing practical examples of how organisations detect, investigate and respond to cyber threats today. Besides the presentations, the event also offered opportunities to network with people interested in cybersecurity and threat intelligence.

Content of the session

During the session, several important concepts related to Cyber Threat Intelligence were discussed. The speakers explained the different types of CTI and how threat intelligence can support active cyber protection.

One of the most interesting parts for me was the demonstration of practical investigations using Censys and pivoting techniques. The presenters showed how indicators such as hashes, fingerprints and CSS files can be used to discover related malicious infrastructure.

Some examples and techniques discussed during the session included:

  • Pivoting on fingerprint.js
  • Using SHA256 hashes and truncating them to 12 characters for searches
  • Searching for malicious infrastructure through shared CSS files such as alert.css
  • Investigating IP addresses using Censys queries
  • Filtering JA4S fingerprints on port 443 and HTTP traffic
  • The importance of avoiding overly strict ASN name filtering to prevent missing results

The speakers also discussed initiatives from the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium such as:

  • Belgian Anti-Phishing Shield
  • Spear Warnings
  • Red Button

These examples demonstrated how CTI is applied in practice to detect phishing campaigns and malicious websites.

Reflection

I found this event very interesting because it directly connected to topics currently covered in my Cyber Threat Intelligence course this semester. Seeing practical demonstrations outside of the classroom helped me better understand how threat intelligence investigations are performed in real environments.

The technical demonstrations with Censys were especially useful because they showed how small indicators can be used to pivot towards larger malicious infrastructures. This made the investigative side of cybersecurity much more tangible.

I also appreciated that the session was accessible for both beginners and people with existing cybersecurity knowledge. Even though some concepts were technical, the explanations remained understandable.

This event confirmed my interest in threat intelligence and cybersecurity investigations. I would definitely attend similar sessions again because practical examples from professionals provide insights that are difficult to obtain through theory alone.
