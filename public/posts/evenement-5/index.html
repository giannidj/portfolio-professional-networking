Tech & Meet – The Birth of NATO’s Cyber Defence

At the beginning of December, I attended a Tech & Meet evening session titled The Birth of NATO’s Cyber Defence, presented by ir. Martin De Pauw.

The session explored how cybersecurity evolved within NATO from a niche technical topic into a full operational domain alongside land, sea and air operations.

About the speaker

Martin De Pauw graduated from the Polytechnic division of the Royal Military Academy in 1989 and initially worked in F16 avionics and air traffic management software. Later in his career, he transitioned into software, hardware and networking environments and eventually became involved in helping shape cyberspace operations within NATO.

After his military career, he continued working at NATO Headquarters in IT and Cyber-related roles.

What made this presentation especially interesting was that the speaker was directly involved in the development of NATO’s cyber defence strategy and structures.

Content of the session

The presentation focused on how NATO gradually recognized cyberspace as a critical operational domain.

One of the key historical moments discussed was NATO officially recognizing cyberspace as a domain of operations in 2016. This decision reflected how cyber threats had evolved from isolated technical incidents into risks capable of impacting international security and critical infrastructure.

Several important cybersecurity topics and examples were discussed during the session, including:

  • the Stuxnet worm and its impact on industrial systems,
  • state-sponsored threat actors such as APT28 (“Fancy Bear”) and APT29 (“Cozy Bear”),
  • insider threats as one of the biggest cybersecurity risks,
  • business continuity during cyber incidents,
  • and the growing role of AI in offensive cyber operations.

I found the discussion around weaponized AI particularly interesting. The speaker explained how AI technologies are increasingly being abused by threat actors, including examples where AI agents were manipulated or jailbroken to assist cyberattacks.

Another important point was the protection of critical national infrastructure such as bridges, power plants and other essential systems. The presentation showed how cyberattacks are no longer limited to stealing data but can potentially impact physical infrastructure and daily life.

Reflection

I found this session extremely interesting because it connected cybersecurity with international politics, military strategy and global security.

Cybersecurity is often approached purely from a technical perspective in courses, but this presentation demonstrated how cyber defence also involves strategy, cooperation between nations and long-term resilience planning.

One message that strongly stood out to me was:

“Cyber has no borders.”

The session clearly showed how interconnected modern societies have become and how cyber threats can affect governments, businesses and civilians simultaneously.

I also appreciated the speaker’s realistic perspective on security. Technical protections are important, but the presentation repeatedly emphasized that people remain one of the weakest links in cybersecurity.

The event reinforced my interest in cybersecurity and threat intelligence, especially in the broader geopolitical and strategic aspects of the field.

Would I recommend it?

Absolutely. I would highly recommend this session to anyone interested in cybersecurity, networking, international security or cyber defence.

The combination of historical context, real-world examples and insider experience made this one of the most interesting Tech & Meet events I attended this year.
