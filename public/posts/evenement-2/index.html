Guest Lecture – Phishing Awareness and Ethical Phishing

Recently, we attended a guest lecture about phishing presented by OutKept. The session focused on phishing awareness, social engineering and the importance of preparing organisations against one of the most common cyber threats today.

Content of the lecture

During the lecture, the speakers explained how phishing remains one of the most effective attack methods used by cybercriminals. Because phishing attacks mainly target human behaviour instead of technical vulnerabilities, awareness and training are extremely important.

OutKept demonstrated their phishing platform and explained how organisations can use phishing simulations and awareness campaigns to better educate employees and reduce the risk of successful attacks.

What I found most interesting was their concept of working with ethical phishers. Through the platform, ethical phishers can create phishing campaigns that are used for awareness and training purposes. If a phishing email used in a campaign receives clicks, the creator earns a bounty.

This creates an interesting incentive model:

- it rewards creativity,

- encourages realistic phishing simulations,

- and helps organisations stay up to date with modern phishing techniques.

The speakers explained that this approach helps them keep pace with malicious attackers, who are constantly improving their social engineering techniques.

Reflection

I found this lecture very relevant because phishing continues to be one of the biggest cybersecurity threats today. Even organisations with strong technical security can still become vulnerable when users are not sufficiently aware of phishing techniques.

The idea of ethical phishing was something I had not encountered before. I thought it was interesting to see how offensive security concepts can also be applied in a constructive and educational way.

The session also highlighted how important the human aspect of cybersecurity is. Technical protections alone are not enough; user awareness and behavioural training are equally critical.

This lecture further increased my interest in social engineering and security awareness topics. It was also interesting to see how companies are building innovative platforms around cybersecurity education and threat prevention.

Another aspect I appreciated was that the lecture was presented by two former Howest students from the same Applied Computer Science bachelor programme. It was motivating to see alumni working professionally in cybersecurity and contributing to innovative initiatives in the field.

Seeing former students build a platform focused on phishing awareness and ethical phishing also made the session feel more relatable. It showed how knowledge and interests developed during the programme can evolve into real-world cybersecurity projects and careers.

Future interest

At the end of the lecture, OutKept mentioned their upcoming Phishathon event. This sounded like a very interesting initiative because it combines creativity, ethical hacking and awareness training in a practical way.

I am definitely considering participating in the next edition, as it seems like a great opportunity to learn more about phishing techniques and improve practical cybersecurity skills.

Would I recommend it?

Yes. I would recommend this lecture to anyone interested in cybersecurity, especially students or professionals interested in phishing, social engineering or security awareness. The topic is highly relevant and the practical examples made the session engaging and easy to follow.
